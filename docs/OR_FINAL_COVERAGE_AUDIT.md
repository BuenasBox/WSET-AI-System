## IDENTIFIED GENUINE GAPS

### Gap 1: Rosé Wine Specification Coverage (RA1 + RA2)

**Evidence:**
- Zero items mention rosé, rosado, or rosé-specific production.
- WSET L3 curriculum covers rosé production (partial maceration, tannin/color balance) and regional styles (Provence, Navarra).

**Impact:** Learners cannot answer rosé-focused exam questions.

**Recommended items:** 2–3
- RA1: Rosé skin contact timing and color/tannin balance (viticulture × vinification)
- RA2: Rosé production choices (whole-bunch vs. destemmed, oak aging, regional style)
- Optional RA5: Rosé service and food pairing

### Gap 2: Service/Pairing/Decanting Underrepresentation (RA5)

**Evidence:**
- Only 4 items in RA5 service category (2.9% of bank)
- Current items: basic service temperature (1), food pairing (2), Port decanting (1)
- Missing scenarios:
  - Mature wine aging: when to drink vs. hold (decision logic)
  - Oxidation vs. fault discrimination (maderization, cork taint, volatility)
  - Glassware choice and aeration strategy
  - Health/safety advice (alcohol content, allergens, histamines)
  - Storage conditions (temperature, humidity, position)
  - Decanting decisions (young Port vs. mature, Burgundy vs. Bordeaux)

**Impact:** RA5 is 12.3% of bank but should be 15–18% (~21–25 items). Deficit of 4–8 items.

**Recommended items:** 4–6
- Mature Vintage Port: aging curve and decanting decision logic
- Burgundy/Bordeaux decanting: style-dependent aeration strategy
- Madeira/Sherry oxidation: maderization vs. fault (sensorial cues)
- Storage condition advice: temperature, light, position (traditional vs. modern)
- Health/allergen advice scenario (for completeness of RA5)
- Wine glass choice: shape/size and its sensorial impact

### Gap 3: SAT-to-Theory Articulation (All RAs)

**Evidence:**
- Zero items explicitly teach SAT structure in relation to production theory.
- Current items touch tasting (23 items) but do not bridge SAT categories (appearance/nose/palate/conclusion) to causal chains.
- Example gap: no item teaches "If red wine shows brownish edge (appearance SAT observation) → implies age/oxidation (production theory) → how does that happen?"

**Impact:** Learners cannot integrate tasting assessment framework with production understanding.

**Recommended items:** 3–4
- SAT appearance cues → production/age theory (browning, opacity, viscosity)
- SAT nose cues → production theory (reductive, malolactic, oak, oxidation)
- SAT palate cues → production theory (acid, tannin, alcohol extraction)
- SAT conclusion cues → quality and aging potential (distinction drivers)

These items should use causal chains as explicit bridges.

### Gap 4: Regional Wine Integration (RA1 + RA2)

**Evidence:**
- Only 11 items integrate regional knowledge (8% of bank).
- Current regional items are scattered (mostly "name regions known for X").
- Missing: region-specific production decisions and how vineyard climate/soil shape wine style.

**Impact:** Regional knowledge remains fragmented; learners cannot synthesize region + style + production method.

**Recommended items:** 2–3
- Vintage variation in classic regions (Burgundy, Bordeaux, Rhône): how does vintage shape production choices?
- Regional style drivers: how does Champagne's chalk soil → production method → style?
- Alternative/emerging regions: how do new regions adapt traditional methods to their climate?
